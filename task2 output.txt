  A: SQL databases utilize indexing and efficient storage structures, such as B-tree or hash trees, to manage large amounts of data effectively.   Q: Can NoSQL databases support complex transactions?
  A: NoSQL databases, designed to handle large amounts of unstructured or semi-structured data, do not inherently support complex transactions out of the box due to their unique schema design and lack of strong consistency guarantees. However, some NoSQL databases like MongoDB, Cassandra, and Couchbase introduce proprietary transaction mechanisms that allow for basic atomicity and isolation, such as "lock-wait" replication patterns, to ensure durability in high-availability environments. Additionally, some modern NoSQL databases provide built-in support for transactions through techniques like "atomic commit" or "distributed transactions," which enable the execution of multiple operations in a single, concurrent transaction.

  Q: Is there a difference between transactional and non-transactional storage in databases?
  A: In databases, transactional storage refers to the use of database management systems (DBMS) that implement atomicity, consistency, isolation, and durability (ACID) principles, ensuring that database transactions are executed as a single, uninterruptible unit. Non-transactional storage, on the other hand, involves storing data in a way that allows multiple transactions to be combined into a single, continuous update operation, often using techniques such as row-level locking or snapshot isolation. In contrast, transactional storage typically employs ACID compliance mechanisms like checkpoints and log files to maintain database consistency between transactions. The primary difference is the level of atomicity and durability provided by each approach.
